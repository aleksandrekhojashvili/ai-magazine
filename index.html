01The Setup

Welcome to college. Your tutor never sleeps.

Artificial intelligence is now a normal part of college life. Students at Drexel use ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Grammarly, and a dozen other tools to write, study, code, brainstorm, and decode confusing readings. For many students, AI feels like a tutor that is always available. It explains tough topics, gives examples, organizes ideas, and helps assignments get started.

But AI also creates a serious question for universities. Is AI helping students learn, or is it making them too dependent? The answer depends on how students use it. If AI helps you understand something better, it can be useful. If you use it to avoid thinking, it can hurt your learning.

This matters at Drexel because students are not just trying to pass classes. They are getting ready for co-op, jobs, and professional life. AI can help with this, but students still need to build their own skills.

Drexel’s AI guidance says students should use AI responsibly and follow the rules of each class (“AI Guidance for Students”). Drexel’s Academic Integrity Policy says students are responsible for understanding when AI is allowed and when it crosses into dishonesty (“Academic Integrity Policy”).

So AI is not just a tech story. It is a story about learning, honesty, and what happens when you walk into your first co-op interview.

To understand what is actually happening, we surveyed 25 students at Penn and Drexel about their AI habits. The results are inside.

02The Upside

AI can help. But it should not replace thinking.

AI is helpful when students use it as support. One of its biggest benefits is that it can explain difficult material in simpler words. If you do not understand a reading, a math problem, a coding concept, or an assignment prompt, AI can give a quick explanation. That is useful when a professor, tutor, or classmate is not around.

Our survey of 25 students showed AI is already part of student life. 48% use AI every day, and 36% use it a few times a week. Most students surveyed use AI regularly, not rarely.

Many students also think AI helps them understand course material. When asked if AI tools help them understand better, 36% picked 5 and 32% picked 4 on a 1 to 5 scale. In written responses, students said AI helps them save time, understand hard topics, organize ideas, and study faster. One student said AI explains math problems “like it’s talking to a kid,” which makes the material easier to follow (Khojashvili, Nasser, and Yumi).

Research by Xiao et al. also supports the idea that AI literacy can help students in higher education. So students should not only be told to avoid AI. They should learn how to use it correctly. AI becomes useful when it helps students understand the work. It becomes a problem when it does the work for them.

03The Catch

The problem is dependence.

The biggest risk is dependence. College is supposed to build critical thinking, independence, discipline, and problem-solving. These skills develop when students struggle with difficult work and learn how to solve problems themselves. If AI does too much of the work, students may finish assignments without actually building those skills.

Our survey shows some students already feel this. When asked if they sometimes feel too dependent on AI, many chose the middle or high numbers. 44% selected 4 or 5 when asked if they put less effort into thinking through problems since using AI regularly (Khojashvili, Nasser, and Yumi). That is nearly half the surveyed students admitting their thinking habits have shifted.

The written responses confirm the issue. Some students said AI made them “lazier.” Another said AI helps them, but it is also hurting their critical thinking (Khojashvili, Nasser, and Yumi). The conflict is right there: students like AI because it is useful and fast, but they also know it can make them less independent.

This concern shows up outside our survey too. RAND found more students using AI for homework, and more students believing it can hurt critical thinking (Schwartz and Diliberti). The Harvard Gazette discusses the worry that AI can weaken independent thinking when people rely on it too much (Mineo). Over-reliance is a real issue, not just a panic.

04Integrity & Careers

Nobody knows where the line is.

Academic integrity is more confusing now

Before AI, cheating was easier to spot. Copying from a classmate, plagiarizing a website, or paying someone to write your essay are clearly dishonest. With AI, the line is not always clear. A student might use AI to brainstorm, fix grammar, write an outline, explain a reading, or generate a paragraph. Some of those uses are fine in one class and forbidden in another.

Some students may break rules without realizing it. They are not trying to cheat. They are guessing. Drexel’s Academic Integrity Policy says to follow the instructor’s rules and ask when unsure (“Academic Integrity Policy”). That is fair, but professors also need to be clear. UNESCO argues schools need clear guidance for generative AI in education so students can use it responsibly (Miao and Holmes).

AI can affect your future career

AI use in college matters after graduation, especially at Drexel where co-op is central. Employers may like students who know AI tools, but they still need workers who can think independently, solve problems, communicate, and make good decisions. In real jobs, problems are not always simple. AI can help, but it cannot replace professional judgment. Students need to know when AI is wrong, biased, or weak. McKinsey explains AI carries real risks: accuracy, bias, privacy, trust (Cheatham, Javanmardian, and Samandari).

So what should Drexel actually do?

Banning AI won’t work. Pretending it’s fine won’t either. Four real moves for the university, and for students.

01

Clear rules for each assignment

Only 20% of students say AI guidance at their school is very clear. Professors should spell out the AI rules per assignment, not just per course. Brainstorming? Outlining? Grammar? Drafts? Code help? Say what is okay and what is not. End the guessing.

For: Professors & Departments
02

Better training, not just warnings

Students in our survey said it themselves: better training, not bans. Schools should teach how to fact-check AI, how to spot bias, when to cite, and how to avoid copying answers you don’t understand. McKinsey notes AI carries real risks. Students need to question it, not just use it.

For: The University
03

Use AI to build skills, not avoid work

There is a real difference between using AI for support and using it as a shortcut. Asking AI to explain a tough concept in simpler words builds learning. Asking AI to write the full answer with no understanding builds nothing. Co-op employers will notice which one you practiced.

For: The Classroom
04

Student responsibility

Even with perfect rules and training, the call is on you. Ask: Is AI helping me understand this, or is it doing the thinking for me? If AI explains, organizes, or critiques, good. If you cannot defend the answer it gave you, that is the line.

For: Every student reading this
→

A full ban would not stop students from using AI. It would just remove support from students who use it to actually understand material. The question isn’t whether to allow AI. It’s how to use it without losing the point of college.

05The Verdict
CONCLUSION
AI should help students grow, not help them avoid the work that creates real learning.

AI is now a major part of college education. It can help students understand difficult topics, save time, and organize their work. For many students, it is already a useful academic tool.

But AI also creates risks when students depend on it too much. The biggest danger is losing critical thinking, independence, and confidence in your own work. AI can also make academic integrity more confusing because students don’t always know what’s allowed.

The goal should be balance. Students should not be afraid of AI, but they should not let it replace their own thinking. Universities like Drexel should give clearer rules and better training so students can use AI responsibly. The point of college is the thinking part. AI should make you better at it, not skip it.

If AI wrote your headline, your essay, or your idea, the question isn’t whether anyone noticed. The question is whether you still know what you actually think.
